OEM REFERENCE GUIDE — CENTRIFUGAL PUMPS
Equipment Class: Centrifugal Pump (applicable to P-101, P-204, P-156, P-089, and similar plant pump assets)
Compiled from general industry maintenance practice for centrifugal pump equipment.

SECTION 1 — SAFETY PRECAUTIONS BEFORE INSPECTION
Before any inspection or maintenance work, power to the pump must be disconnected and locked out per
standard lock-out/tag-out procedure. Suction and discharge valves should be closed, the pump drained, and
any hazardous residue neutralized before opening the casing.

SECTION 2 — ROUTINE INSPECTION SCHEDULE
- Daily: Visual walkaround for leakage at pipe joints and mounting brackets; listen for abnormal noise
  suggestive of cavitation (a rattling or "marbles in the pump" sound) or high-pitched bearing squeal.
- Weekly: Log discharge pressure and flow rate. A drop of roughly 10% or more from baseline typically
  indicates internal wear, impeller damage, or suction-side clogging.
- Monthly: Inspect and clean motor cooling fins; debris accumulation is a common cause of heat-related
  bearing failure.
- Quarterly: Check foundation and mounting bolts, and piping supports, for vibration-induced loosening.

SECTION 3 — MECHANICAL SEAL CONDITION AND FAILURE PROGRESSION
Mechanical seals should be checked via the housing's inspection point:
- Dry condition: seal is functioning normally.
- Intermittent dripping: early indication of seal face wear; maintenance should be scheduled soon,
  not treated as an emergency yet.
- Continuous/steady leakage: seal has effectively failed; replacement is required immediately, before
  fluid reaches and damages the motor bearings.

Common root causes of premature seal failure include:
- Running the pump dry, even briefly, which is one of the most damaging conditions for mechanical seals.
- Operating fluid temperature or chemical composition outside the seal material's rated tolerance.
- Abrasive solids settling near the seal during idle/shutdown periods ("abrasive lag"), often prevented
  by flushing the pump before extended downtime.

SECTION 4 — IMPELLER AND CASING INSPECTION
During any teardown or seal replacement, the impeller and casing (volute) should also be inspected for:
- Erosion or pitting on impeller vanes (a honeycomb-like surface pattern), typically caused by cavitation
  or abrasive fluid content.
- Excessive clearance between impeller and casing due to wear, which reduces pump efficiency and
  discharge head.
- Debris lodged in impeller passages, which can cause vibration and hydraulic imbalance.

SECTION 5 — COMMON FAILURE MODES AND LIKELY CAUSES (troubleshooting reference)
- Excessive heat: commonly caused by dead-heading (running against a closed discharge valve) or
  bearing friction from inadequate lubrication.
- Rapid/repeated seal wear: often linked to abrasive lag during shutdowns, or seal material incompatible
  with process fluid chemistry.
- Cavitation noise/vibration: usually indicates insufficient Net Positive Suction Head (NPSH) available,
  a clogged suction strainer, or a suction-side restriction.

SECTION 6 — EXTENDED SHUTDOWN / STORAGE GUIDANCE
If a pump will be out of service for more than approximately 30 days:
1. Drain and flush all process fluid from the casing.
2. Apply a light protective oil coating to internal cast iron components to prevent rust-binding.
3. Manually rotate the shaft by hand periodically (roughly monthly) to prevent seal faces from bonding
   and to redistribute bearing lubrication.
